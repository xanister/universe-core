Notes from Beneath the Veil

Being a practical discourse on the Restless Dead for the benefit of those who would avoid or (failing that) survive them.  
Set down in Gaard, in the late reign of Meliora—whom the whispering streets call the Pale.

I am told I do not exist. That is the safest lie the Crown and Temple have ever told. If necromancers are myths, there need be no trials, and if there are no trials, there can be no martyrs. Yet the law—that careful scripture of denials—names my crimes with exquisite precision. Make of that contradiction what you will.

I write because I have watched too many good people die twice: once on the field, and again as someone’s instrument. If you are a soldier, a midwife, a sexton, a night watchman on the river stairs—if you are anyone who must labor within arm’s length of death—this is for you.

What follows is not a grimoire. You will find no rites here—only the truths a citizen requires to recognize the signs, keep their life, and keep the peace of the dead.

\---

I. On Zombies — Shackled Flesh

What they are.  
A zombie is a body without a will. Not a spirit bound into meat (that is a darker art), but meat given the crude illusion of will. Think of a dropped puppet that yet twitches because a careless god left one string tied.

How they behave.  
Slow. Tireless. Unafraid. Their hands bruise and break; their teeth worry at living throats like dogs at bone. They do not learn. They do not plan. If ordered to hold a door, they will hold that door until the house is ash and the door is a frame of char.

What they heed.  
A zombie answers a master’s simple compulsion—guard, kill, carry—and little else. Deprive it of orders, and it stands. Strike it, and it responds like a mill horse corrected with a goad: not with anger, but with movement.

What thwarts them.

Distance and light. A walking man with his wits outpaces three. A lantern and a little courage will spare you many panics.

Severing and fire. Break knees and elbows; cut tendons; burn what remains.

True sanctity. A relic carried by a heart that believes may unmake the binding. (A symbol waved by a coward will only tire the arm.)

Fetters. They snag—on chains, briars, prayer‑flags, the clutter of a peasant yard. Disorder is the ally of the living.

Field mark.  
The breath that is not breath: a damp rattle like wind through a bottle, on lips that do not frost in winter. If you see it, back away. If you smell it first, you are already too near.

A caution to soldiers.  
Do not leave your dead untended in places where sorcery has trod. A corpse is a door with a hinge already hung.

II. On Wraiths — The Starved Shade

What they are.  
A wraith is will without body: a soul peeled loose from the Veil by murder, betrayal, oath‑breaking—or by those who study men like me and fail at the final reckoning. They are most often born by accident; they may be bound by intention, but seldom made on purpose without ruin to the maker.

How they behave.  
They cling to the site of their sundering, or to an object that remembers them (a blade, a ring, a cradle). Their touch steals the warmth that marks the living. Not blood. Not breath. Years. A single grasp can lay winter upon a man’s bones.

They are cunning within a narrow compass. Do not expect speech; do expect the idea of speech—cold thoughts pressed into your head like a thumb on wax: give back, go away, kneel.

What thwarts them.

Sun. Daylight thins them. It rarely ends them.

Iron spoken for the dead. A blade that has killed to defend the helpless, and was afterward blessed, answers them better than silver or gold.

Ritual light. Not any lamp, but the kind set for the keeping of vigil—oil consecrated and tended by a steady hand. The light does not harm the shade; it reminds the room of morning. That is often enough to force a wraith to fold and flee the reach of its own memory.

Truth. Resolve the injustice that anchors it—return the stolen child’s bones; name the murderer; break the oath with better oath. Wraiths are lawsuits argued in the tongue of winter. Settle the case, and the court adjourns.

Field mark.  
Frost‑fern crawling up—not down—the inside of window glass; candles guttering with no draft; the taste of old copper at the back of the throat.

A caution to the curious.  
Bindings that hold a wraith also wound the living. Draw such circles with your feet, not your chalk, and the stake you hammer must be the truth you are prepared to speak aloud.

III. On Liches — The Crown of Rot

What they are.  
A lich is not an accident. He is a scholar who loved his breath more than the world, and bartered the warm parts of life—taste, sleep, the ease of tears—for continuance. He removes his name from his breast and locks it in a vessel; so long as that vessel endures, his body may be hacked apart and will rise again, mending itself to the measure of his memory.

How they behave.  
Cold, patient, exquisitely polite until they are not. They collect. Books. Cities. Families. The notion of “mine” in them grows until it covers the horizon. Lesser dead gather to them as crows to a gallows.

What thwarts them.

The Vessel. Call it phylactery, soul‑jar, omen‑box. Find it or you will fight forever. It will not sit in a silver dish under a bell. It will be a hinge in a door you never think to open, a jewel in a saint’s reliquary, a word in a prayer you cannot unsay.

Time denied. Starve them of apprentices and libraries. Burn the roads that lead to their cellars of thought. A lich is a plan that wears bones; confound the plan, and the bones become merely bones.

Consecrated ruin. Holy ground unsettles them, not by burning, but by refusing to remember who they are. In such places their spells forget themselves.

Field mark.  
Silence where there ought to be vermin. Flies avoid their chambers. Dogs will not eat meat gone cold in their kitchens.

A final word.  
Most kings fear the lich’s armies. The wiser fear their treaties. A lich’s promise is iron until it serves him to melt the ore.

IV. On Vampires — The Noble Hunger

What they are.  
Not corpses; not shades; not living. They are changed, and the change is elegant. The first of them dressed well for the end of the world and have refused to undress since.

How they behave.  
They are strong and swift, senses sharpened like razors. They hunt as cats do—not for hunger alone, but for aesthetic. Some love the crowd and the candle‑lit hall; some prefer the cellar and the rain barrel. The older they are, the more the night bends around them: voices find your ear from a room away; doors open for them as if by embarrassment at being closed.

What they need.  
Blood. Not gallons. Enough. Hunger is pain, it is music, it is prayer turned inward until only the self remains. They do not breathe or eat, but they may pretend both to be pleasing. They do not sleep by law, but by prudence: sunlight scours them. The young will char and fail; the old—those ancient aristocrats—may step beneath dawn a few breaths longer, and then the light will teach them humility all the same.

What thwarts them.

Sun and flame. The only final teachers.

Field mark.  
Mirrors do not lie and neither do they tell all. You will see them there, but less. The glass does not adore them as flesh does.

A kindness.  
Yes, sometimes they love. No, it never saves anyone for long.

V. On Law, Belief, and the Useful Myth

The Lex Gaardium forbids corpse‑binding, soul‑traffic, and dealings that unseat a man from his allotted place in the wheel. Good. These prohibitions keep villages whole and funerals clean. Keep them. Obey them. And know this: the Crown and Temple also cultivate disbelief. It is easier to govern a people who think monsters are stories than a people who know exactly what stalks their lanes.

If you must choose between the comfort of that myth and the plain fact of what stands at the end of your lane, choose the fact. Then choose your feet, and run.

\---

VI. Simple Counsels for the Living

Carry a real light when you keep watch: oil you trust, trimmed by your own hand.

Bury your dead with names and witnesses. What is named is harder to misuse.

If travel takes you by battlefields or ruins, go at noon, not dusk.

Keep a length of stout cord and a hook; zombies tangle, wraiths waver, and a vampire’s cloak is not immune to a fisherman’s wit.

Do not interrogate the night for its secrets. It will answer, and your life is too short already.

\---

I leave you this with no hope of pardon and no desire for applause. If they hang me, let it be for speaking plainly. If you live, let it be because you read this and believed—not in me, but in your own eyes.

— The Lanternbreaker
