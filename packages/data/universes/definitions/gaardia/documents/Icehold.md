ICEHOLD

A Crown‑Faith Gazetteer Entry for Officers of the North March

Compiled in the late reign of Queen Meliora I (“the Pale”), 1466–1467 4A.

\---

I. Situation & Fabric

Site. The Icehold is a fortification cut into a tide‑ground glacier at the windward lip of the Eluscion Sea. Its upper wards occupy a natural bowl of blue ice and metamorphic rock; its lower works descend through frost‑planed galleries into an older warren of vents, culverts, and bolt‑ladders. The main approach is a cliff stair and hoist cradle on the seaward face; secondary approaches are service louvres—slatted weather covers over vent mouths—used to admit or exhaust air and steam.

Origin. Gaardian surveyors found tool marks within the deepest galleries that do not match current state patterns. Bolt‑ladders of pre‑Gaardian make suggest the place began as a cold‑store and drainage work, later grown into a refuge during lean winters. The oldest chambers (“the hexhouse” by guard slang) show soot glazing from oil‑fired lamps despite minimal airflow, implying prolonged occupation in darkness.

Phenomena. Accounts from early crews record “cold breath” in still corridors, frost ferns growing up panes, and the failure of common lamps below the salt vaults. These signs match field marks for wraith activity (a starved shade tied to a site of sundering); standard mitigations in such places are vigil‑lamps of consecrated oil, iron borne in defense of the helpless, and the settlement of whatever truth anchors the unrest.

\---

II. Mysterious History

Whispers & interdictions. In the late 4th Age, Crown inspectors recorded folk prohibitions against burying the violent dead near the glacier and warned wardens not to abandon corpses in thaw rooms. Such cautions align with common Gaardian practice in regions touched by unlawful arts: unattended corpses are “doors with hinges already hung,” and the Lex Gaardium empowers Crown‑Faith officers to suppress signs of necromancy. The anonymous grave‑scholar hunted by the Crown‑Faith Tribunal—styled “the Lanternbreaker” in seized pamphlets—explicitly warns soldiers and sextons to keep ritual light, to sever and burn corrupted flesh, and to settle causes of haunting when possible.

Collapsed chapelry. A bricked nave found below the S‑blocks holds hooks for vigil‑lanterns and a rust‑eaten iron rail; its altar stone is blank. No royal marks. The fittings imply a watch‑chapel rather than a parish: a place to tend the dead and keep the peace of the tunnels.

\---

III. Present Status: Makeshift Mine & Gaol (1460s 4A)

Mandate. After coastal depots on the Eluscion rim were shuttered for economy, the Icehold was recommissioned as a combined holding prison and works site. Its civil function is to quarry stone and ice for local works, lift magnetite from thin lenses in the wall rock, and—seasonally—boil brine in steam pans for salt. Its penal function is to confine Crown prisoners and those remanded by provincial courts pending transport.

Plant & works.

Pressure galleries. Steam mains run from kiln rooms to thaw icefalls, de‑ice hoists, and scour foul air. Relief shutters (“blinds”) can be hand‑spiked or hauled by chain.

Vent run. A crawling‑height inspection way alongside the main mains; used by fitters to chase leaks and by guards on checks.

Sluices. Numbered chutes for meltwater and tailings (S‑1 through S‑6).

Purge. A safety and riot‑control procedure: open shutters in sequence to vent, then blow steam through a block to clear miasma or force abandonment. When used improperly, purges scald and asphyxiate; posted orders require a watch‑priest to attend with vigil‑lamps when a purge touches chapelry levels.

Labour. Convict work gangs rotate between hoist, pan room, and adit, with cold‑rest in sixes. Common injuries are scald, white‑lung, and crush. Chaplaincy keeps “last‑watch” rites for the tunnels, both to honor the dead and—per standing order—to check wraith‑signs and set lights where men fear to stand. These measures follow Crown‑Faith practice for sites with suspected restless dead.

Recent disturbance. On 23–24.09.1466 4A, a veteran prisoner, Korr Brightblade, escaped custody during maintenance at S‑4; subsequent reports describe interference with purge sequences and fighting in the pressure gallery. His case is now cited in warden schools as an example of how access to vent runs and blinds multiplies a single prisoner’s leverage.

\---

IV. The Gaardian Stewards

Office‑holders. Administration is shared between Crown and Temple under the Stewardry of the Icehold.

Warden of Works (Crown). Oversees hoists, shutters, steam plant, and ore quotas. Keeps the purge book and certifies that blinds and chains answer.

Provost Marshal (Crown). Discipline and muster rolls; custody of keys; movement orders for gangs and escorts.

Watch‑Priest (Temple). Chapelry, rites of last watch, and duty to maintain vigil‑lamps in suspect levels; advises on signs of profanation or unrest among the dead. Guidance on wraith encounter protocol is drawn from standard grave‑scholar cautions: ritual light to “remind the room of morning,” iron spoken for the dead, and truth‑making where injustice anchors the shade.

Quartermaster (Crown‑Temple). Rations, lamp oil, salt, and wool issue; special charge to keep sanctified oil for vigils in sealed tins.

Law & custom. The Crown‑Faith Tribunal claims competence over necromantic crimes at the Icehold; reports of “cold breath,” extinguished lamps, or handling of corpses contrary to rite are to be forwarded to the Tribunal’s clerk with the Warden’s seal. Public pamphleteers call this overreach; the Tribunal answers that the Lex Gaardium speaks plainly where the dead are troubled.

Marks & colors. The Stewardry seal shows a slatted louvre over a lamp—symbol of air kept moving and lights kept. Guard sashes are winter‑blue with an iron‑grey edge.

\---

V. Notes for Officers

Lamp discipline. Ordinary lamps gutter in the lower vaults; a watch‑priest’s vigil‑lamp is to lead any party beneath S‑4. (Field guidance: a relic borne by a believer unbinds better than a symbol waved by a coward.)

Body handling. No unguarded dead in thaw rooms. Mark and burn contaminated flesh quickly; record in the purge book if a purge coincided with a death.

Shutter drills. Never spike a relief blind without a second hand on the chain and a third at the lamp. If a purge is ordered, post the chaplain at the far louvre so the corridor “remembers” morning.

\---

Glossary (site usage)

Louvre. A weathering cover of fixed or adjustable slats set over a vent to shed snow and break wind while allowing air (or steam) to pass.

Vent run. Low maintenance way running parallel to a main duct or drain.

Pressure gallery. Chamber where mains and blinds are worked; also used as a choke‑point under guard.

Purge (verb). To clear a block with staged venting and steam; as a noun, the ordered sequence itself.
